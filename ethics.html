Ethical Use of Artificial Intelligence

Responsible Use in the Academe

  • Navigating the AI landscape requires parents and students to become responsible users by focusing on key skills. This involves understanding the technology's algorithmic basis, promoting ethical use to avoid pitfalls like echo chambers, and ensuring students apply critical thinking to question and verify AI-generated information. Ultimately, the goal is to integrate AI into the curriculum so students are prepared to use this powerful tool effectively and ethically in their future.

Avoiding Plagiarism and Misuse

  • Plagiarism, the use of another's work without proper acknowledgment, remains unethical and sometimes illegal, necessitating an updated understanding for both authors and readers. Navigating the blurred lines of information requires authors to master proper citation practices for quotations and paraphrasing, and, especially with AI, to verify all external content while crediting original sources. Ultimately, utilizing good note-taking, advanced plagiarism checkers, and consistent attribution safeguards both ethical integrity and the validity of one's work.

Accuracy and Verification

  • AI accuracy measures how often a model's predictions match the actual outcomes. It's calculated as the ratio of correct predictions to total predictions, usually expressed as a percentage. If a model correctly identifies 90 out of 100 spam emails, it has an accuracy rate of 90%.
  • How to verify if something is AI generated? Inconsistencies and repetition: Occasionally, AI produces nonsensical or odd sentences which can be a clear indicator of AI-generated text. Abrupt shifts in tone, style, or topic can point to AI that is struggling to maintain coherent ideas.

Bias and Fairness

  • AI fairness means ensuring that systems operate impartially and equitably, avoiding unjustified adverse effects on any individual or group based on protected characteristics like race or gender. The opposite, AI bias, consists of systematic errors leading to skewed or unfair outcomes, often introduced because the training data is unrepresentative or reflects historical human prejudices. Consequently, achieving fairness requires actively designing, training, and deploying models that eliminate these biases to ensure widespread trust and responsible AI adoption across critical sectors.
